Investigation

Investigating the Brief

Forests play an important role in maintaining ecological balance. They filter water, store carbon, and produce oxygen through photosynthesis. These functions help regulate climate and support biodiversity. However, forests are increasingly threatened by environmental risks such as wildfires, which are a major cause of forest loss worldwide [1].

Research shows that forest fires have been increasing in frequency and severity in recent years, damaging ecosystems and releasing large amounts of carbon into the atmosphere [3]. Wildfires are influenced by changing environmental conditions over time. For example, rising temperatures, dry air, and low moisture levels can create conditions that allow fires to start and spread more easily.

Environmental indicators such as humidity, temperature, and sunlight often change gradually, but rapid changes in these conditions can significantly increase the risk of fire. Monitoring these factors over time can therefore help detect when forests are becoming more vulnerable [2].

Modelling is a suitable approach to explore wildfire risk because many environmental variables interact to influence fire behaviour. A model can combine several variables and simulate how risk changes as environmental conditions vary. By using environmental data, a model can estimate the likelihood of wildfire occurrence and help provide early warnings before fires begin.

Existing Solutions

One existing system is the NASA Fire Information System, which uses satellite imagery to detect and monitor fires around the world. Its purpose is to track wildfire locations and activity in real time. The system gathers satellite data such as heat signatures and fire locations and uses rule-based risk modelling to estimate fire danger. However, satellites can only observe a location when passing overhead, which creates time gaps in the monitoring process.

Another solution uses unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) equipped with optical and thermal cameras. These drones collect environmental data such as humidity, temperature, and heat patterns in forests. The data is used in predictive or rule-based models to estimate wildfire risk in specific areas. A key advantage is that UAVs can monitor areas more frequently than satellites. However, their flight duration is limited, and they can be vulnerable to damage during operation [4].

A third system is developed by OroraTech, which provides advanced wildfire monitoring and prediction services. The system uses satellite data, geographic information, and environmental variables to simulate fire spread using mathematical and rule-based modelling. It can generate forecasts for several hours ahead. However, this approach requires significant computing power and is primarily designed for commercial use [5].

How Research Informed My Project

Because my research showed that environmental conditions strongly influence wildfire risk, I decided to focus on this environmental issue. Studies showed that variables such as temperature, humidity, and light intensity affect how easily fires can start and spread, so I chose these variables as the main data inputs for my system.

Because research also showed that fire risk often increases when environmental conditions change rapidly, I decided to simulate situations where temperature or dryness rises over time. Modelling these changes allows the system to estimate increasing risk rather than simply detecting fires after they occur.

Because many existing systems use rule-based modelling to combine environmental variables, I decided to adopt a similar rules-based approach. This type of model allows different environmental factors to contribute to an overall risk score in a simple and interpretable way.

Final Idea

My final project idea is to create a system that monitors environmental conditions and uses a model to estimate the risk of wildfire in a specific location.

A limitation of this approach is that the system can only represent conditions in the local area where the data is collected.
