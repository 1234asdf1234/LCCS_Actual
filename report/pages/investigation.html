Investigating The Brief

Forests support critical ecological balance by filtering water, providing oxygen through photosynthesis, etc.

Wildfires have been increasingly pervasive factor in causing forest loss [1]. They have received great attention throughout the world and being monitored in a variety of ways [2].

People have been estimating the probability of wildfires using different models. Some private institutions manage to calculate “Fire radiative power” for the area at concern, i.e., speed of fuel consumption, to predict the risk. Global collaboration on the topic is also taking place, which provides information such as burnt areas and real-time fire reports, to conclude a set of fire forecasts [3].

Some environmental factors such as moisture affect fire risk directly, by providing suitable conditions for flames to occur. Other factors, such as light, imply these factors, e.g., lower light intensity may be cause by clouds, potentially leads to cooler and wetter environments.

I have been intrigued to investigate wildfire risks by using both kinds of environmental factors, e.g., moisture, light, temperature to simulate the probability of wildfire happening in one specific area. I will then build a rule-based model to summarise both environmental and artificial factors in relation to wildfire risks.

Existing Solutions

NASA has provided a fire information system. It uses satellite data to gather the distribution, duration and other key information of wildfires in real time. Then, it uses rule-based modelling to predict fire risks. E.g., places near detected fires are more likely to be ignited. It provides an accurate and comprehensive view of wildfire status. However, it may not respond in time, as satellites can only scan when they are above the target area, creating temporal time gaps.

Apart from satellites, Unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) equipped with optical or thermal cameras are also used to detect and model fire risk. These vehicles can not only monitor factors such as humidity but also move in specially designed routes to get data from more areas. Similarly, they also use rule-based modelling to predict the risk in selected areas. A great advantage is its temporal flexibility, allowing surveillance with least latency. But they also suffer from low flight durations (approx. 3hrs) and risk of being damaged [4].

Private tech companies such as Ororatech have created complex models which simulate the fire spread. It includes many advanced factors such as elevation from satellite data and geographical maps, uses a range of mathematical models and rule-based models to give detailed predictions. One big advantage is that it provides hourly forecast up to 12 hours. However, it is aimed commercially, meaning it requires substantial calculating power and a high price [5].

Research Informed Decision

As my research showed, we need a solution with frequent monitoring of environmental factors and requires less calculating power. I decide to collect temperature, humidity and light intensity data from the environment, as they are easy to be monitored. 

I am inspired to simulate a situation where heat rises rapidly. This comes from the NASA satellite map that numerous fires occur in low-latitude areas, where heat-rising situations are prevalent. 

I chose rule-based modelling as my research showed various influences the environmental factors have on wildfires. A range of simple rules can generally represent all these influences. 

Final Idea

I decided to build an embedded system and a model which measures several tangible factors of wildfires, to predict the risk and warn the users.

One limitation is that the embedded system can only monitor and reflect status of a limited area.
